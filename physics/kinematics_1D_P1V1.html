Motion in 1D — Foundations Kinematics · Physics

Core Formula Box

Average velocity: $$\vec{v}_{avg} = \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}$$

Average speed: $$\text{avg speed} = \dfrac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}$$

Instantaneous velocity: $$v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0}\dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = \text{slope of tangent on } x\text{-}t \text{ graph}$$

Instantaneous acceleration: $$a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2} = \text{slope of tangent on } v\text{-}t \text{ graph}$$

Equations of motion (constant $a$ ONLY): $$v = u + at \qquad s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \qquad v^2 = u^2 + 2as \qquad s_n = u + \dfrac{a}{2}(2n-1)$$

Area under $v$-$t$ graph = displacement (signed, can cancel). Sum of $|$areas$|$ = total distance.

Key Concepts (max 15)

  1. Distance is a scalar path length, always ≥ displacement magnitude; displacement is a vector, straight-line net change.
  2. Average speed uses total distance; average velocity uses net displacement — they are equal only for straight-line, one-direction motion.
  3. Round-trip trap: if a body returns to start, displacement = 0 → average velocity = 0, but average speed ≠ 0.
  4. Instantaneous velocity is the limit of average velocity as $\Delta t \to 0$; it is the slope of the tangent, not the slope of a chord.
  5. On an $x$-$t$ graph, a straight line ⇒ uniform velocity; a curve ⇒ velocity is changing (non-uniform motion).
  6. For a curved $x$-$t$ graph, velocity at any instant = slope of the tangent drawn at that exact point, not the average slope of the whole curve.
  7. Concave-up $x$-$t$ curve ⇒ increasing slope ⇒ positive acceleration. Concave-down ⇒ deceleration or reversing.
  8. The three equations of motion apply ONLY when acceleration is constant (magnitude and direction both fixed).
  9. Sign convention must be fixed before starting a problem: pick one direction positive, stick with it for $u$, $v$, $a$, $s$ throughout.
  10. On a $v$-$t$ graph, slope = acceleration, and the graph is a straight line only for constant $a$.
  11. Area under $v$-$t$ graph between two times = displacement in that interval (signed — area below the axis is negative).
  12. When velocity changes sign (crosses the $t$-axis) on a $v$-$t$ graph, the body reverses direction: total distance = sum of absolute areas of each segment, but net displacement = algebraic sum.
  13. To convert $x$-$t \to v$-$t$: read the slope at each point. To convert $v$-$t \to a$-$t$: read the slope at each point.
  14. To convert $v$-$t \to x$-$t$: accumulate signed area (this is why area under $v$-$t$ is displacement, not distance, unless velocity never changes sign).
  15. $s_n$ (distance in the $n^{th}$ second) is a discrete formula — only valid for constant acceleration, and gives distance covered during that one specific second, not the position.

Common Mistakes (top 5 — full detail in Tab 4)

  • Using displacement formulas when velocity changes sign mid-motion (forgetting to split at the reversal point).
  • Treating average speed and average velocity as always equal.
  • Reading the slope of a curved $x$-$t$ graph as if it were a straight line (using end-point slope instead of tangent at the point).
  • Applying $v=u+at$ etc. to a case where acceleration is not constant (e.g., $a$ depends on $t$, $x$, or $v$).
  • Losing track of sign convention halfway through a problem, especially after a direction reversal.
Exam trick: Whenever a $v$-$t$ graph crosses the time axis, immediately mark that crossing time. Compute area separately above and below the axis. Distance = sum of magnitudes; Displacement = signed sum. This single habit prevents ~80% of sign-error losses in this topic.
Exam trick: On a curved $x$-$t$ graph question, if asked for velocity "at $t = t_0$", draw the tangent only at that point — never join two distant points on the curve and call that slope the instantaneous velocity.

Memory Hooks

  • "Distance is what the odometer reads, displacement is what the GPS reads."
  • SUVAT constant-a check: before writing any of the three equations, ask "is $a$ constant here?" — if no, go back to calculus ($v=dx/dt$, $a=dv/dt$).
  • Slope-of-tangent, not slope-of-chord — say this out loud for every curved-graph velocity question.

5 Must-Do PYQs (answers only)

Q1 (Practice question, style-matched, source unverified): A body travels from A to B at 40 km/h and returns from B to A at 60 km/h along the same straight path. Find average speed for the whole trip.

Answer: 48 km/h (harmonic mean, since equal distances).

Q2 (Practice question, style-matched, source unverified): Position of a particle is $x = 2t^2 - 3t + 1$ (SI units). Find instantaneous velocity at $t=2\,s$.

Answer: $v = 4t - 3 \Rightarrow v(2) = 5$ m/s.

Q3 (Practice question, style-matched, source unverified): A particle starts with $u = 10$ m/s and decelerates at $2\ \text{m/s}^2$. Find total distance covered in the first 12 s (it reverses direction).

Answer: Stops at $t=5\,s$ after covering 25 m forward, then reverses for 7 s covering 49 m back ⇒ total distance = 74 m; displacement = 25 − 49 = −24 m.

Q4 (Practice question, style-matched, source unverified): An $x$-$t$ graph is a parabola opening upward. Is the particle's speed increasing or decreasing?

Answer: Increasing — slope (velocity magnitude) grows steadily on an upward parabola, meaning constant positive acceleration.

Q5 (Practice question, style-matched, source unverified): A $v$-$t$ graph shows velocity going from +6 m/s to −4 m/s linearly over 5 s. Find both total distance and net displacement.

Answer: Crosses zero at $t=3\,s$. Area(0–3s) = ½·3·6 = 9 m; Area(3–5s) = ½·2·4 = −4 m. Distance = 9+4 = 13 m; Displacement = 9−4 = 5 m.

$$\vec{v}_{avg} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t}$$

$\Delta \vec{x}$: net displacement (vector)
$\Delta t$: total time elapsed

When to use: any time you need average velocity over an interval, regardless of whether motion is uniform.

Don't confuse with: average speed (uses distance, a scalar, in the numerator).

Derivation logic: direct definition — net change in position over elapsed time.

$$\text{avg speed} = \dfrac{\text{total path length}}{\Delta t}$$

total path length: sum of all distances traveled, direction ignored

When to use: whenever the path is not a straight one-directional line (round trips, reversals).

Don't confuse with: average velocity — equal only if motion never reverses direction.

Derivation logic: scalar analogue of average velocity; distance replaces displacement.

$$v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}$$

$v$: instantaneous velocity
graphically: slope of the tangent to the $x$-$t$ curve at that instant

When to use: velocity "at an instant" or "at a point" — especially for curved (non-uniform motion) $x$-$t$ graphs.

Don't confuse with: average velocity, which is slope of the chord joining two points, not the tangent at one point.

Derivation logic: limit of $\Delta x/\Delta t$ as $\Delta t \to 0$.

$$a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}$$

graphically: slope of tangent to $v$-$t$ curve

When to use: instantaneous acceleration, especially when $a$ itself is not constant.

Don't confuse with: average acceleration = $\Delta v/\Delta t$ over a finite interval.

Derivation logic: rate of change of velocity; second derivative of position.

$$v = u + at$$

$u,v$: initial, final velocity
$a$: constant acceleration

Condition: $a$ must be constant in both magnitude and direction over the interval.

Don't confuse with: using it when $a = f(t)$ or $a = f(v)$ — must integrate instead.

Derivation logic: direct integration of $a = dv/dt$ with constant $a$.

$$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$

$s$: displacement in time $t$

Condition: constant $a$; $s$ is signed (can be negative if net motion is backward).

Don't confuse with: distance travelled when the body reverses direction within time $t$ — this formula gives net displacement only.

Derivation logic: integrate $v=u+at$ with respect to $t$.

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Condition: constant $a$; useful when time is not given/needed.

Don't confuse with: applying it across a direction reversal without splitting the motion into segments.

Derivation logic: eliminate $t$ between $v=u+at$ and $s=ut+\tfrac12at^2$.

$$s_n = u + \dfrac{a}{2}(2n-1)$$

$s_n$: distance covered in the $n^{th}$ second (not total distance up to $n$ seconds)

Condition: constant $a$, $n$ is an integer.

Don't confuse with: $s = ut+\tfrac12at^2$, which gives position/displacement up to time $t$, not distance in one specific second.

Derivation logic: $s_n = s(n) - s(n-1)$.

$$\text{Area under } v\text{-}t \text{ curve} = \int v\,dt = \Delta x$$

When to use: converting a $v$-$t$ graph into displacement or distance.

Don't confuse with: if $v$ changes sign, the signed area gives displacement; the sum of absolute areas of each sign-segment gives distance.

Derivation logic: definite integral of velocity is the fundamental theorem of calculus applied to $v=dx/dt$.

Solved Pattern Examples

Pattern: Curved x-t graph — instantaneous velocity at a point

Practice question (style-matched, source unverified): Position of a particle is given by $x = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$ (m, s). Find its velocity at $t=1\,s$ and state whether it is speeding up or slowing down.

Step 1 — differentiate to get v(t): $v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$. Why: the x-t graph here is curved (cubic), so velocity is the slope of the tangent at each instant, not a single average slope — we must differentiate, not just divide $\Delta x/\Delta t$.

Step 2 — evaluate at t=1: $v(1) = 3-12+9 = 0$ m/s. Why: this is a specific instant, so we plug $t=1$ into the tangent-slope function, not into a chord between two far-apart points.

Step 3 — find acceleration to judge speeding up/slowing down: $a = dv/dt = 6t-12 \Rightarrow a(1) = -6\ \text{m/s}^2$. Why: since $v=0$ at this instant and $a\ne0$, the particle is momentarily at rest and about to reverse — this is the graph's turning point.

Pattern: v-t graph with sign change — distance vs displacement

Practice question (style-matched, source unverified): A particle's velocity varies as: $+8$ m/s at $t=0$, decreasing linearly to $-6$ m/s at $t=7\,s$. Find total distance and net displacement over 7 s.

Step 1 — find when v=0: slope $= \dfrac{-6-8}{7} = -2\ \text{m/s}^2$. $v(t) = 8-2t = 0 \Rightarrow t=4\,s$. Why: this is the reversal point — the graph crosses the time axis here, so distance and displacement calculations must be split at this instant.

Step 2 — area from 0 to 4s (positive region): triangle, $\tfrac12 \times 4 \times 8 = 16$ m. Why: this is genuine forward distance since velocity stayed positive throughout this segment.

Step 3 — area from 4 to 7s (negative region): triangle, $\tfrac12 \times 3 \times 6 = 9$ m, but this area is below the axis so it is $-9$ m in displacement terms. Why: negative velocity means the particle is moving backward — this segment reduces net displacement but adds to total distance.

Step 4 — combine: Total distance $= 16+9 = 25$ m. Net displacement $= 16-9 = 7$ m. Why: distance sums magnitudes of every segment; displacement algebraically sums signed areas — this is exactly the trap examiners test.

High-Yield Sub-topics (priority order)

  1. Distance vs displacement / avg speed vs avg velocity — appears almost every year across all three exams, easy marks if the round-trip trap is internalized.
  2. v-t graph area with sign change — very high yield, moderate difficulty, the single biggest mark-loser in this topic if not practiced.
  3. SUVAT equations (constant a) — foundational, feeds directly into projectile motion and later chapters.
  4. x-t graph slope reading (uniform and curved) — essential prerequisite for Advanced-level multi-graph questions.
  5. Graph inter-conversion (x-t ↔ v-t ↔ a-t) — lower frequency alone but underlies almost every Advanced kinematics problem.

1. Treating average speed = average velocity by default

Looks like: student computes $\Delta x / \Delta t$ and calls it "average speed."

Why it happens: in single-direction motion the two are numerically equal, so the distinction gets forgotten until a round-trip problem appears.

Correct version: always ask "did the path double back?" If yes, compute distance and displacement separately.

Trips up: Mains, WBJEE (direct-plug questions with a hidden round trip).

2. Using the two-point chord slope as "instantaneous velocity" on a curved x-t graph

Looks like: student picks two points on a curved graph, computes $\Delta x/\Delta t$ between them, and reports it as velocity "at" one of those points.

Why it happens: works fine for straight-line (uniform motion) graphs, and the habit carries over incorrectly to curves.

Correct version: instantaneous velocity is the tangent slope at that single point; for a curve, differentiate $x(t)$ or draw the tangent geometrically.

Trips up: Advanced (graph-heavy, curved x-t questions).

3. Applying SUVAT equations when acceleration is not constant

Looks like: student uses $v=u+at$ even though $a$ is given as a function of $t$, $x$, or $v$.

Why it happens: SUVAT is memorized as "the" motion formula without checking the constant-a condition first.

Correct version: if $a$ is variable, go back to $v=dx/dt$, $a=dv/dt$ and integrate/differentiate directly.

Trips up: Advanced (frequently sets variable-a traps).

4. Treating area under v-t graph as distance even when velocity changes sign

Looks like: student computes one big triangle/trapezoid area across a sign change and reports it as "total distance."

Why it happens: the "area = distance" shortcut is true for one-directional motion, and the reversal case isn't flagged.

Correct version: split the graph at every point where $v=0$ (axis crossing); sum absolute areas for distance, signed areas for displacement.

Trips up: Mains, Advanced, WBJEE — this is the single most-tested trap in the whole topic.

5. Losing sign convention after a direction reversal

Looks like: student flips the sign of velocity after reversal but forgets to also check the sign of acceleration/displacement consistently.

Why it happens: no single fixed positive direction was chosen at the start of the problem.

Correct version: fix one direction as positive before writing a single equation, and never change it mid-solution — represent reversal only through the sign of $v$, not by redefining axes.

Trips up: all three exams, especially multi-stage motion problems.

6. Confusing $s_n$ (distance in nth second) with $s$ up to time $n$

Looks like: student uses $s = ut+\tfrac12at^2$ with $t=n$ when the question actually asks for distance covered during the $n^{th}$ second only.

Why it happens: surface similarity between the two formulas and quantities.

Correct version: use $s_n = u + \tfrac{a}{2}(2n-1)$ specifically for "distance in the nth second" questions.

Trips up: WBJEE, Mains (direct formula-recall questions).

7. Misreading concavity on an x-t graph

Looks like: student assumes a curving x-t graph always means increasing speed, regardless of concavity direction.

Why it happens: not connecting "rate of change of slope" (second derivative) to concavity visually.

Correct version: concave-up (opening upward) with increasing slope ⇒ speeding up in positive direction; concave-down flattening slope ⇒ decelerating; slope becoming negative ⇒ reversal.

Trips up: Advanced (graph interpretation without explicit equations).

8. Forgetting that displacement can be negative but distance never can

Looks like: student reports a negative value as "distance travelled."

Why it happens: carrying over the signed result from a displacement/area calculation without taking magnitude.

Correct version: distance is always reported as a positive scalar; only displacement retains sign.

Trips up: Mains, WBJEE (numeric-answer questions where sign errors cost the mark).
